Provisional Birth and Death Data

The Health Department collects data on every birth and death that happens in New York City. These data, called vital statistics, provide a fundamental understanding of birth trends, death trends, and population health in New York City.

The data on this page are provisional data, which means that the numbers may change as the Health Department continues to process the data. Publishing provisional data allows the Health Department to release data faster, while it undergoes additional processing like quality review and finalization. Final data are published in the Annual Summary of Vital Statistics. Final data are also available online at EpiQuery.

Birth characteristics

Method Term Birth weight Coverage

Usually, about two-thirds of births in NYC are vaginal births. Other methods include cesareans (c-sections) and vaginal birth after cesarean (VBAC), when somebody gives birth vaginally after previously giving birth by c-section.

About 90% of births in NYC are babies born at term - 37 weeks of gestation or older. Pre-term births are when the baby is less than 37 weeks of gestation. A subset of pre-term births are very pre-term births, which are when the baby is less than 32 weeks of gestation at time of birth.

About 90% of births in NYC are babies with a normal birthweight - 2,500 grams and above (about 5.5 pounds). Babies are considered low birthweight if they weigh less than 2,500 grams. A subset of low birthweight babies are considered very low birthweight, at less than 1,500 grams.

Births are categorized as covered by Medicaid, or non-Medicaid. Usually, a little more than half of births are covered by Medicaid.

Care characteristics

These indicators of health and care provided during pregnancy or after birth are key to understanding birth outcomes.

Pre-pregnancy diabetes Pre-pregnancy hypertension Prenatal care Breastfeeding

Usually, over 1% of mothers have pre-pregnancy diabetes, which can be a risk factor for adverse birth outcomes.

Usually, under 3% of mothers have pre-pregnancy hypertension, which can be a risk factor for adverse birth outcomes.

About 70% of mothers receive prenatal care starting in the first trimester. This can be a protective factor against adverse birth outcomes.

Usually, almost 90% of infants are breastfed. This can benefit the health of mothers/parents and babies. This information is typically collected in the first few days after birth, so these numbers represent any breastfeeding that happened during that period.

Death Data

Data on deaths come from death certificates. The Health Department tracks information about all deaths that occur in New York City, even among people who are not residents of NYC. The two death sections below include quarterly provisional data on infant mortality and deaths among all ages, with details on demographics and causes of death.

Infant mortality rates are the number of infant deaths (less than 1 year of age) per 1,000 live births. Due to the small number of infant deaths, rates are prone to fluctuations. All infant mortality data are shown as four-quarter rolling averages: one quarter's data averaged with the data from the three previous quarters.

There is usually a higher neonatal infant mortality rate - deaths among infants less than 28 days old. Deaths are post-neonatal if the baby is at least 28 days old, but less than one year old. Additional Resources

All data here are provisional, which means that they are not finalized and are subject to change. Data on births and deaths can take months or years to be finalized as additional details are reported, deaths are investigated by the Office of Chief Medical Examiner, and results from autopsy or toxicology results are completed. Once data for a given year are complete, Health Department staff spend time processing and analyzing the data. Typically, data are finalized about two years after the end of a reporting year.

These provisional data are being released to provide more timely information on births and deaths in NYC. When using or disseminating these data, we recommend flagging them as provisional and subject to change.
